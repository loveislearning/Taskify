Common Formulas

  • Area of Circle: \( A = \pi r^2 \)
  • Quadratic Formula: \( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)
  • Pythagoras Theorem: \( a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \)
  • Simple Interest: \( SI = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100} \)
  • Compound Interest: \( CI = P \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^T - P \)
  • Slope of a Line: \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)
  • Distance Formula: \( d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \)
  • Newton’s Second Law: \( F = m \cdot a \)
  • Ohm’s Law: \( V = I \cdot R \)
  • Speed: \( v = \frac{d}{t} \)
  • Perimeter of Rectangle: \( P = 2(l + w) \)
  • Area of Triangle: \( A = \frac{1}{2} b h \)

Grammar Rules (Quick Reference)

  • Capitalize the first word of a sentence and proper nouns.
  • Use a semicolon, not a comma, before “hence” when it introduces an independent clause.
  • Spell out numbers one through nine, except in titles or when followed by a unit.
  • Use “a number of” with a plural verb: “A number of points are...”
  • Capitalize nouns followed by numerals: “Experiment 2,” “Table 6.”
  • Use “fewer” for countable nouns, “less” for uncountable nouns.
  • Place punctuation inside quotation marks in American English.
  • Use “who” for people, “which/that” for things.
  • Use the Oxford comma in lists for clarity.
  • “Its” is possessive; “it’s” means “it is.”
  • Use “affect” as a verb, “effect” as a noun (usually).
